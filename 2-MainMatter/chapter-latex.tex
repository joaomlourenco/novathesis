%%=======================================================================
\chapter{A Short \hologo{LaTeX} Tutorial with Examples}
\label{cha:a_short_latex_tutorial_with_examples}
%%=======================================================================

This chapter is a practical guide to writing your thesis with \hologo{LaTeX} and the \novathesis{} template.
It covers the most common tasks: structuring your document, formatting text, inserting figures and tables, typesetting equations, managing citations, and producing the final PDF.

Use these examples as a starting point.
The \href{https://www.overleaf.com/learn}{Overleaf documentation} and \href{https://tex.stackexchange.com}{TeX~Stack~Exchange} are excellent resources for additional help.

\begin{warningbox}[title={Do not edit “\texttt{template.tex}” or “\texttt{novathesis.cls}”}]
  All your customizations go into the files inside \texttt{0-Config/}.
  Your chapter content lives in \texttt{2-MainMatter/} and is listed in \texttt{0-Config/4\_files.tex}.
  The root file \texttt{template.tex} and the class file \texttt{novathesis.cls} are \textbf{never} edited directly.
\end{warningbox}

%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Adding a New Chapter}
\label{sub:adding_chapter}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Create a new \texttt{chapter-mywork.tex} file in \texttt{2-MainMatter/}, then register it in \texttt{0-Config/4\_files.tex}:

\begin{latexcode}
\ntaddfile{chapter}{chapter-mywork}
\end{latexcode}

Inside the file, start with a \latexinline{\chapter} command and give it a label:

\begin{latexcode}
\chapter{My Work}
\label{cha:my_work}

\section{Overview}
\label{sec:overview}
\end{latexcode}

The sectioning hierarchy is:
\latexinline{\chapter} \(\to\) \latexinline{\section} \(\to\) \latexinline{\subsection} \(\to\) \latexinline{\subsubsection} \(\to\) \latexinline{\paragraph} \(\to\) \latexinline{\subparagraph}.
Avoid going deeper than \latexinline{\subsection} unless absolutely necessary.


%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Cross-references}
\label{sub:cross_references}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Place a \latexinline{\label} immediately after the element you want to reference — a chapter, section, figure, table, or equation — and then use \latexinline{\autoref} to cite it:

\begin{latexcode}
See \autoref{cha:my_work} for details.
The results are in \autoref{fig:results} and \autoref{tab:results}.
\end{latexcode}

\latexinline{\autoref} automatically prepends the correct word (\emph{Chapter}, \emph{Figure}, \emph{Table}, \ldots) in the current language.
Use \latexinline{\ref} if you only want the number.

\begin{highlightbox}[title={Tip: non-breaking space before \textbackslash ref}]
  Always write \latexinline{Figure~\ref{fig:x}} with a non-breaking space (\latexinline{~}) so the word and number never split across lines.
\end{highlightbox}

%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Teaching \texttt{\textbackslash autoref} about a new object type}
\label{ssub:autoref_new_type}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\latexinline{\autoref} looks up the label prefix to determine the word to prepend.
When you define a new numbered environment — say, a \texttt{listing} or \texttt{algorithm} — you register its name by defining the macro \latexinline{\<counter>autorefname}:

\begin{latexcode}
% In 0-Config/5_packages.tex, after \usepackage{algorithm2e}:
\newcommand{\algorithmautorefname}{Algorithm}

% For a custom theorem environment with counter "theorem":
\newcommand{\theoremautorefname}{Theorem}
\end{latexcode}

If your document is multilingual you can conditionally define the name based on the active language:

\begin{latexcode}
\addto\extrasenglish{\renewcommand{\algorithmautorefname}{Algorithm}}
\addto\extrasportuges{\renewcommand{\algorithmautorefname}{Algoritmo}}
\end{latexcode}


%%=======================================================================
\section{The \texttt{cleveref} Package}
\label{sec:cleveref}
%%=======================================================================

The \texttt{cleveref} package is a more powerful alternative to \latexinline{\autoref}.
Load it \emph{last} in your preamble (in \texttt{0-Config/5\_packages.tex}):

\begin{latexcode}
\usepackage{cleveref}
\end{latexcode}

\begin{warningbox}[title={\texttt{cleveref} must be loaded last}]
  \texttt{cleveref} must be loaded \emph{after} \texttt{hyperref} and after any package that defines new float or theorem environments.
  In the \novathesis{} template, add it at the end of \texttt{0-Config/5\_packages.tex}.
\end{warningbox}


%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Basic Usage}
\label{sub:cref_basic}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \caption{Core \texttt{cleveref} commands.}
  \label{tab:cref_commands}
  \centering
  \begin{ShadedTabular}{lp{0.6\linewidth}}
    \toprule
    Command & Result \\
    \midrule
    \latexinline{\cref{key}}         & Lowercase: \emph{figure~1}, \emph{chapter~2}, \ldots \\
    \latexinline{\Cref{key}}         & Capitalised: \emph{Figure~1}, \emph{Chapter~2}, \ldots \\
    \latexinline{\cref{k1,k2,k3}}    & Multiple refs in one call: \emph{figures~1, 2 and~3} \\
    \latexinline{\crefrange{a}{b}}   & Range: \emph{figures~1 to~3} \\
    \latexinline{\labelcref{key}}    & Number only (no type word) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{ShadedTabular}
\end{table}

Use \latexinline{\Cref} at the start of a sentence and \latexinline{\cref} elsewhere:
\begin{latexcode}
\Cref{cha:my_work} presents the proposed approach.
The results are summarised in \cref{tab:results,fig:results}.
\end{latexcode}

A key advantage over \latexinline{\autoref} is that \latexinline{\cref} can handle a
comma-separated list of labels and automatically formats them — even across different
object types — as a single, grammatically correct phrase.


%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Teaching \texttt{cleveref} about a New Object Type}
\label{sub:cref_new_type}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

For every new numbered environment you create, register its singular and plural names
with \latexinline{\crefname} (lowercase) and \latexinline{\Crefname} (capitalised):

\begin{latexcode}
% In 0-Config/5_packages.tex, after defining the environment:
\crefname{algorithm}{algorithm}{algorithms}   % \cref{}
\Crefname{algorithm}{Algorithm}{Algorithms}  % \Cref{}

% Custom theorem environment:
\crefname{theorem}{theorem}{theorems}
\Crefname{theorem}{Theorem}{Theorems}
\end{latexcode}

The third argument is the \emph{plural} form used when multiple labels are listed in
a single \latexinline{\cref{k1,k2}} call.

For multilingual documents, wrap the definitions in language hooks:
\begin{latexcode}
\addto\extrasenglish{%
  \crefname{algorithm}{algorithm}{algorithms}
  \Crefname{algorithm}{Algorithm}{Algorithms}
}
\addto\extrasportuges{%
  \crefname{algorithm}{algoritmo}{algoritmos}
  \Crefname{algorithm}{Algoritmo}{Algoritmos}
}
\end{latexcode}

\section{Equations}
\label{sec:equations}
%%=======================================================================

\hologo{LaTeX} excels at mathematical typesetting.
Inline formulas are delimited by \latexinline{$...$}, for example the discriminant is $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$.

Display-style (unnumbered) equations use \latexinline{\[...\]}:
\[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \]

Numbered equations use the \texttt{equation} environment and are referenced with \latexinline{\autoref}:
\begin{latexcode}
\begin{equation}
  E = mc^2
  \label{eq:einstein}
\end{equation}
As shown in \autoref{eq:einstein}, energy and mass are equivalent.
\end{latexcode}
\begin{equation}
  E = mc^2
  \label{eq:einstein}
\end{equation}
As shown in \autoref{eq:einstein}, energy and mass are equivalent.

For multi-line aligned equations, use the \texttt{align} environment:
\begin{latexcode}
\begin{align}
  f(x) &= x^2 + 2x + 1 \label{eq:fx}\\
       &= (x+1)^2      \label{eq:fx_factored}
\end{align}
\end{latexcode}

\section{Tables}
\label{sec:tables}
%%=======================================================================

Professional tables follow these rules:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Never use vertical lines.
  \item Place the caption \emph{above} the table.
  \item Use \latexinline{\toprule}, \latexinline{\midrule}, and \latexinline{\bottomrule} from \texttt{booktabs}.
  \item Use \texttt{xltabular} for tables that may span multiple pages.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{latexcode}

    \toprule
    Method   & Precision & Recall \\
    \midrule
    Baseline & 0.72      & 0.68   \\
    Proposed & 0.89      & 0.91   \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}
\end{latexcode}

\section{Figures}
\label{sec:figures}
%%=======================================================================

Store image files in \texttt{5-Figures/}.
Always prefer \emph{vector} formats (PDF) over \emph{bitmap} formats (PNG, JPG):
vector images scale without quality loss, while bitmap images become pixelated when enlarged — compare \autoref{fig:snowman-bitmap} and \autoref{fig:snowman-vector}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[height=1in]{snowman-bitmap}
  \includegraphics[height=3in]{snowman-bitmap}
  \caption{Bitmap image (PNG/JPG): quality degrades at larger sizes.}
  \label{fig:snowman-bitmap}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[height=1in]{snowman-vectorial}
  \includegraphics[height=3in]{snowman-vectorial}
  \caption{Vector image (PDF): scales perfectly at any size.}
  \label{fig:snowman-vector}
\end{figure}

A minimal figure:
\begin{latexcode}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{my-diagram}
  \caption{A clear, descriptive caption.}
  \label{fig:my_diagram}
\end{figure}
\end{latexcode}

The placement specifier \latexinline{[htbp]} means: try \textbf{h}ere, then \textbf{t}op, then \textbf{b}ottom, then a float \textbf{p}age.
Avoid \latexinline{[H]} (from the \texttt{float} package) — it defeats \hologo{LaTeX}'s float algorithm and causes large white gaps.

Only references actually cited in the text appear in the bibliography list.

\subsection{Image Formats}
\label{sub:image_formats}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

All three engines accept \textbf{PDF}, \textbf{PNG}, and \textbf{JPG} directly.
Use PDF for diagrams and plots, PNG for screenshots with transparency, and JPG for photographs.
Avoid EPS — convert to PDF first with \bashinline{epstopdf}.


%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Algorithms}
\label{sub:algorithms}
%%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

To typeset algorithms, add to \texttt{0-Config/5\_packages.tex}:
\begin{latexcode}
\usepackage{algorithm2e}
\RestyleAlgo{ruled}
\end{latexcode}

And optionally list algorithms in the front matter via \texttt{0-Config/4\_files.tex}:
\begin{latexcode}
\ntaddlistof{listofalgorithms}
\end{latexcode}

% \begin{algorithm}
% $i\gets 10$\;
% \eIf{$i\geq 5$}{$i\gets i-1$\;}{\If{$i\leq 3$}{$i\gets i+2$\;}}
% \caption{Example algorithm.}
% \end{algorithm}

% \printbibliography[heading=subbibliography, segment=\therefsegment, title={\bibname\ for chapter~\thechapter}]
